Vietnam War Flashcards (Detailed)

Who was Ho Chi Minh?
He was the communist leader of North Vietnam. He wanted to unify Vietnam under a communist government and was a key figure in the fight against French, Japanese, and later American influence in Vietnam.
Who were the Vietcong?
The Vietcong were South Vietnamese communist rebels who used guerrilla tactics to fight against the South Vietnamese government and U.S. forces. They were supported by North Vietnam.
Who were the Vietminh?
The Vietminh were a national independence coalition led by Ho Chi Minh that fought against French colonial rule in Vietnam and later against Japanese occupation during World War II.
Who was Robert McNamara?
He was the U.S. Secretary of Defense during the Vietnam War under Presidents Kennedy and Johnson. He was a major architect of the U.S. involvement in Vietnam.
What were Doves and Hawks?
Doves were people who opposed the Vietnam War, believing it was morally wrong or unwinnable. Hawks supported the war, wanting to defeat communism through military force.
Who was Walter Cronkite?
A respected CBS news anchor who, after the Tet Offensive, publicly expressed doubt that the Vietnam War could be won, influencing public opinion.
Who was Richard Nixon?
He was the 37th President of the U.S. who introduced 'Vietnamization'—the process of withdrawing U.S. troops and handing over combat to South Vietnamese forces.
Who was Lyndon B. Johnson?
He was the 36th President of the U.S. who escalated U.S. involvement in the Vietnam War after the Gulf of Tonkin incident.
What was the Ho Chi Minh Trail?
A network of jungle paths and tunnels through Laos and Cambodia used by North Vietnam to transport troops and supplies to the Vietcong in South Vietnam.
What happened in the Gulf of Tonkin?
In 1964, U.S. ships were allegedly attacked by North Vietnamese forces. This led Congress to pass the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution, giving the president broad military powers.
What happened in Saigon on April 30, 1975?
North Vietnamese forces captured Saigon, marking the end of the Vietnam War. The city was later renamed Ho Chi Minh City.
What was the 1963 Coup?
A military coup in South Vietnam overthrew and assassinated President Ngo Dinh Diem, with tacit approval from the U.S., due to his unpopular leadership.
What was the Tonkin Gulf Resolution?
A 1964 congressional resolution that allowed President Johnson to use military force in Vietnam without an official declaration of war.
What were the Geneva Accords?
A 1954 agreement that temporarily divided Vietnam at the 17th parallel into North (communist) and South (non-communist) and called for elections to unify the country.
What was the Tet Offensive?
A surprise attack by North Vietnamese and Vietcong forces on South Vietnamese cities during the Vietnamese New Year. It was a military failure for the North but a psychological victory.
What happened in the 1968 Election?
Richard Nixon was elected president, promising 'peace with honor' and a plan to end the Vietnam War.
What was the My Lai Massacre?
A 1968 event where U.S. soldiers killed hundreds of unarmed Vietnamese civilians, mostly women and children, in the village of My Lai.
What was the Kent State Massacre?
In 1970, National Guard troops shot and killed four students during an anti-war protest at Kent State University in Ohio.
What is the Domino Theory?
The belief that if one country in a region fell to communism, neighboring countries would soon follow, like a row of dominoes.
How is imperialism related to Vietnam?
Vietnam was a French colony (part of French Indochina). Many Vietnamese resisted imperial rule, leading to the rise of independence movements.
How did Americans feel about the war at the start and later?
At first, many supported it to stop communism. As the war dragged on and casualties rose, public support declined sharply.
What is Napalm?
A flammable gel used in bombs by the U.S. that stuck to surfaces and caused severe burns. It was controversial due to its horrific effects on civilians.
What is Agent Orange?
A toxic herbicide used by the U.S. to destroy jungle cover. It caused long-term health problems, including cancer and birth defects.
How was the morale of U.S. soldiers?
Often poor, due to unclear goals, difficult jungle warfare, and growing public opposition at home.
How did TV affect the war?
Television showed graphic images of the war and protests, contributing to the American public's growing disapproval.
What is the Credibility Gap?
A term used to describe the public's distrust of the government due to conflicting information about the war's progress.
How did the war affect the Great Society?
Funding and attention shifted from domestic social programs to the war effort, weakening Lyndon Johnson’s anti-poverty initiatives.
Who was drafted and how?
Young American men, especially from working-class backgrounds, were selected by a lottery system to serve in the military.
What is a conscientious objector?
Someone who refuses military service due to moral or religious beliefs against war.
Who opposed the war?
Students, civil rights leaders, veterans, and religious groups protested against U.S. involvement in Vietnam.
What did Martin Luther King Jr. think of the war?
He opposed it, calling it unjust and harmful to the poor and the civil rights movement.
What is Vietnamization?
A policy introduced by Nixon to gradually withdraw U.S. troops and shift combat responsibility to South Vietnamese forces.
Who were the Silent Majority?
Americans who did not publicly protest but supported the government's war policies.
What were the Pentagon Papers?
Leaked secret documents showing the U.S. government had misled the public about the Vietnam War’s progress and decisions.
What is the War Powers Act?
A 1973 law requiring the president to get Congressional approval within 60 days of deploying military forces.
What were the results of the Vietnam War?
North Vietnam won, the country was unified under communism, and many Americans lost trust in their government.
How many people died in the war?
About 58,000 U.S. soldiers and over 2 million Vietnamese civilians and soldiers died.
